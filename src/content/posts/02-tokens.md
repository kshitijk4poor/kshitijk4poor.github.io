When we talk about "discrete tokens," we're referring to individual words or pieces of text that are separate and distinct from each other. For example, in the sentence "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog," each word is a discrete token: "The," "quick," "brown," and so on.

Now, when we say we're converting these tokens into "continuous vectors," think of it like transforming each word into a unique set of numbers. These numbers represent various aspects or features of the word, such as its meaning, context, or relationships with other words.

This transformation is what we call "embedding." It's like taking each word and encoding it into a multidimensional space where similar words are located closer to each other. So, instead of just having individual words, we now have these numerical representations (vectors) that capture more information about the words and how they relate to each other.

By converting discrete tokens into continuous vectors, we enable machines to better understand and work with text data because these vectors contain richer information about the words and their meanings. This allows us to perform various tasks, like text classification or sentiment analysis, more effectively.
